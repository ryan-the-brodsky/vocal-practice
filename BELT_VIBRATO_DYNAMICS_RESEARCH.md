# Focused Research — Dynamics, Vibrato, Belt & Style (build-ready exercises)

> Deep-research workflow, adversarially verified. Companion to EXERCISE_TAXONOMY_RESEARCH.md — fills the 3 coming-soon capability categories.

## Executive Summary

All three capabilities are build-ready to different degrees. BELT & STYLE has the strongest primary-source exercise specs: the Roll (2016) Journal of Voice study of four Broadway master teachers yields exact, transcribable bright-vowel syllables ('nyah'/njæ on descending five-note patterns, /ma/→/mæ/ alternating arpeggios, 'wee'/wi descending arpeggios, 'yay'/jei alternating, 'way'/wei descending arpeggio) — all on clear scorable vowels with concrete pitch shapes, plus a physiologically grounded twang mechanism (aryepiglottic/epiglottic-funnel narrowing reinforcing 2–4 kHz, cued via 'nyah'/duck sounds) corroborated by Estill, CVT, and EMG work. The chest-dominant-vs-high-mix debate resolves to a graded TA/adduction continuum (Kochis-Jennings et al.) where low/mid belt is chest/TA-dominant and high belt above C5 shifts to a head-voice-dominated mix with closed/bright vowels and very little vibrato. VIBRATO is build-ready as a scorability-flagged capability: rate norms (~5 Hz classical, 5–7 Hz standard) and trainable suppression (straight-tone-into-vibrato) are well-evidenced, but the app's mean-cents scoring will average over or misread the pitch oscillation, so vibrato exercises should run as follow-along or carry an explicit limitation note. DYNAMICS & CONTROL (messa di voce) is robustly defined but NOT measurable by this app: every primary source confirms MDV is loudness (SPL ~18.5 dB swing) modulation on a stationary pitch, which a pitch-only scorer cannot measure — build it as an unscored follow-along with a loudness caveat.

### 1. [high, vote 3-0 / 2-1]

BELT SET-UP EXERCISES (build-ready, pitch-scorable): Four concrete, citable belt vocalises from Broadway master teachers, all on clear vowels with transcribable scale-degree patterns. (1) NYAH DESCENDING FIVE-NOTE — pattern [7,5,4,2,0] (5-4-3-2-1 descending), syllable 'nyah' /njæ/, the bright nasalized vowel adds twang/aryepiglottic narrowing for belt brightness; descending; PITCH-SCORABLE (clear sustained vowel). (2) MA/MÆ ALTERNATING ARPEGGIO — pattern [0,4,7,4,0] (1-3-5-3-1), syllable /ma/ shifting to /mæ/ as the singer climbs toward/through C5, the bright /æ/ vowel modification is the documented belt set-up; both/ascending through range; PITCH-SCORABLE. (3) WEE DESCENDING ARPEGGIO — pattern [7,4,0] (5-3-1) or [12,7,4,0], syllable 'wee' /wi/, the closed /i/ vowel keeps the high belt narrow and forward (head-voice-dominated mix above C5); descending; PITCH-SCORABLE. (4) YAY ALTERNATING — pattern [0,4,7,4,0], syllable 'yay' /jei/ or 'way' /wei/ (Charles used 'way' on a descending arpeggio), closed-ish /ei/ vowel; PITCH-SCORABLE. Source: Roll, 'The Evolution of the Female Broadway Belt Voice' (Journal of Voice 2016, PMID 26296854) — verbatim teacher exercises.

### 2. [high, vote 3-0 / 2-1]

TWANG DRILLS (build-ready, pitch-scorable with caveat): The twang mechanism is narrowing of the aryepiglottic sphincter / epiglottic funnel (the tissue ring between arytenoids and epiglottis), which reinforces harmonics in the 2,000–4,000 Hz band to produce a bright, carrying sound. CUE: 'nyah'/duck/witch sounds (CVT explicitly says 'as if imitating a duck'). Concrete drill: a 'nyah' /njæ/ five-note descending scale [7,5,4,2,0] or a 'nyah' arpeggio [0,4,7,4,0], emphasizing the bright nasal twang. PITCH-SCORABLE on the clear /æ/ vowel — but the app scores pitch, not the 2–4 kHz spectral brightness that IS the twang target, so surface a note that the app verifies pitch accuracy, not twang quality itself.


**Evidence:** Claim [20] (3-0, high): voicescience.org grounded in Yanagisawa & Estill 1989 + 2024 MRI study — 'Narrowing the tissue ring between the arytenoid cartilages and the epiglottis reinforces harmonics in the 2,000–4,000 Hz range, producing a bright, carrying quality'; 'nyah'/duck is the standard uncontested Estill teaching cue. Claim [4] (2-1, high): CVT primary source — 'the epiglottic funnel is narrowed' when twanging; the funnel sides ARE the aryepiglottic folds, so CVT twang = Estill aryepiglottic narrowing. Claim [3] (3-0): CVT Edge 'distinct twang of the epiglottic funnel (as if imitating a duck).' Claim [2] (3-0): Edge's sharper aryepiglottic-fold narrowing IS the twang mechanism. Scorability caveat: app measures cents, not the 2–4 kHz boost.


**Sources:** https://www.voicescience.org/lexicon/estill-voice-training/ · https://completevocalinstitute.com/complete-vocal-technique/ · https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199716302181


### 3. [high, vote 3-0 / 2-1]

BELT MECHANISM / WHAT TO CUE: Belt is best modeled as a graded continuum of thyroarytenoid (TA) engagement + vocal-fold adduction rather than a binary. LOW/MID belt is chest/TA-dominant ('chestmix' — produced by increasing TA activity and adduction of the vocal processes). HIGH belt (above C5) shifts to a head-voice-dominated MIX with closed/bright vowels, forward 'narrow' resonance, and very little vibrato (speech-like). CVT operationalizes belt as two distinct modes with different laryngeal gestures: OVERDRIVE (full-metallic, chest-dominant set-up) vs EDGE (more constricted, brighter/twangier, lighter/sharper). This supports building TWO belt set-up exercises: a chesty Overdrive-style belt and a brighter twangy Edge-style belt.


**Evidence:** Claim [18] (3-0, high): Kochis-Jennings et al. EMG — TA activity and adduction increase monotonically head→headmix→chestmix→chest at same pitch, particularly at higher frequencies (graded continuum). Claim [19] (3-0): chestmix high-midrange produced by increasing TA + adduction (chest-dominant mix physiology). Claims [15][16] (2-1, high): high belt above C5 mixes head voice, closed vowels, very little vibrato. Claims [0][1][2][3] (3-0): CVT four modes; Overdrive vs Edge are perceptually + physiologically distinct laryngeal gestures (McGlashan/Sadolin 2016/17). Claim [21] (3-0): Estill belt = M1/chest-dominant + AES narrowing. The debate resolves register-dependent, not as a single answer.


**Sources:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199710002195 · https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199716302181 · https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199715001563 · https://cvtresearch.com/vocal-modes/general/ · https://www.voicescience.org/lexicon/estill-voice-training/

### 5. [high, vote 3-0]

VIBRATO — FREEING & CONTROL EXERCISES (build-ready with scorability flag): Vibrato is a real, voluntarily trainable skill — singers measurably reduce both rate and extent on demand (straight-tone), confirming straight-tone-into-vibrato and delayed-onset control exercises target a trainable capability. CONCRETE EXERCISES: (1) STRAIGHT-TONE-INTO-VIBRATO — sustain a single pitch [0] straight, then release into vibrato on the second half; trains control. (2) DELAYED VIBRATO ONSET — sustained [0] held straight for ~2 beats then vibrato. (3) For FREEING vibrato, relaxation/pulsing drills ('ha-ha-ha' diaphragmatic pulses, jaw pulses) on a sustained pitch. MECHANISM/CUE: target ~5–7 Hz rate (classical clusters ~5 Hz; standard definition 5–7 undulations/sec, ±~1 semitone extent). SCORABILITY: vibrato is a pitch OSCILLATION of ±~1 semitone — the app's mean-cents scoring will average it toward the center pitch OR misread it as instability. RECOMMENDATION: score the STRAIGHT-TONE portion for pitch accuracy (that part IS a stable single pitch and is scorable); run the VIBRATO portion as follow-along; surface a note that the app cannot grade vibrato rate/regularity, only the straight-tone center pitch.


**Evidence:** Claim [5] (3-0, high): Nix et al. 2016 multicenter study — classical college majors' best-classical vibrato rates 4.6–5.4 Hz (~5 Hz), corroborated by Seashore (1931)/Sundberg (1994) 5–7 undulations/sec ±~1 semitone, and cross-genre rock ~5.0 Hz. Claim [6] (3-0): both rate AND extent measurably reduced in the nonvibrato condition vs habitual/best-classical — voluntary controllability confirmed (true 'straight' is minimal, not zero). Claims [7][8] (3-0): mean-metric vibrato analysis is valid only for uniform/persistent classical vibrato and is inadequate for CCM/MT/jazz; CCM vibrato has higher coefficient of variation (less regular) — directly cautions that mean-cents scoring will misread a non-uniform CCM vibrato. The ±1 semitone extent = ±100 cents oscillation, exactly the kind of swing the app's stable-portion mean-cents scoring would average out or flag as unstable.


**Sources:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199715002064 · https://www.jvoice.org/article/S0892-1997(25)00094-3/fulltext


### 6. [high, vote 3-0]

DYNAMICS & CONTROL — MESSA DI VOCE (build-ready as UNSCORED follow-along only; NOT pitch-scorable). DEFINITION/EXERCISE: sustain a SINGLE pitch [0] on one clear vowel (e.g. /a/ or /i/) while performing a continuous crescendo from near-silence to maximum loudness, then a symmetric decrescendo back to near-silence (a 'swell'). Suggested ~slow, e.g. 8–12 beats total, symmetric halves. Companion drills: controlled soft onset/offset on a sustained [0]. MECHANISM/CUE: the trained variable is LOUDNESS (SPL — empirically ~18.5 dB swing), driven by breath/appoggio support; pitch is held essentially constant by definition. SCORABILITY: the app scores mean cents (pitch), not loudness in any model, so it CANNOT measure the actual MDV target. RECOMMENDATION: run as UNSCORED FOLLOW-ALONG and surface an explicit note ('this exercise trains volume control on a steady pitch — the app guides you but cannot grade loudness'). A secondary use: because pitch is MEANT to stay stationary, the app COULD optionally flag incidental pitch drift (singers tend sharp on crescendo / flat on decrescendo) as a faults-only signal — but that measures a side-effect, not the target.


**Evidence:** Claims [9][13] (3-0, high): MDV = sustaining a single pitch with linear crescendo to max then decrescendo to silence, time-symmetric (J Voice S0892199714002458; Titze et al. 1997 PMID 10335642) — 'the only feature that should change is the volume, not the pitch.' Claims [11][12] (3-0): PLOS ONE 2025 — all subjects performed MDV on a stationary pitch (f0 ~121 Hz male / ~245 Hz female, ±1 Hz across slow/fast) with median SPL extent 18.5 dB(A) — intensity is the trained dimension. Claims [10][14] (3-0): loudness/SPL is a distinct measurement axis from pitch; the app scores cents only (CLAUDE.md confirms 'mean cents over the stable part') with a −45 dB RMS gate (not a loudness-envelope scorer), so it cannot measure the MDV target — this is a design choice, not a physical impossibility (other apps do amplitude scoring).


**Sources:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199714002458 · https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0325284 · https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10335642/


### 7. [high, vote 3-0 / 2-1]

CONTESTED POINTS TO SURFACE TO THE USER: (1) BELT chest-dominant vs high-mix — genuinely register-dependent: Estill/CVT-Overdrive frame belt as M1/chest-dominant (TA-dominant + AES narrowing), while Broadway master-teacher findings put HIGH belt (above C5) as head-voice-dominated mix; not a single answer. (2) ESTILL vs CVT framing of belt/twang — same physiology, different taxonomy: Estill treats twang as an isolable aryepiglottic 'Figure'; CVT routes belt through Overdrive/Edge modes; both agree supraglottic narrowing produces the 2–4 kHz boost. (3) Whether aryepiglottic narrowing is INDEPENDENT sphincter action or driven by posterior tongue-root movement — active 2024 debate (MRI), but does not change the cue or the spectral result. (4) VIBRATO taught directly vs allowed to emerge — the verified science shows it is voluntarily controllable/trainable, but mean-metric analysis presumes a classical-uniform vibrato that does not fit CCM. (5) The Overdrive/Edge laryngeal study (McGlashan/Sadolin 2016) was co-authored by CVT's commercial founder (self-validation caveat) and is n=20, unreplicated.


**Evidence:** Claim [1] verifier flags the Sadolin conflict-of-interest (CVI commercially sells CVT; study validates own taxonomy; n=20, unreplicated) and that Estill frames the same gestures differently. Claims [15][16] verifiers note belt registration is register-dependent (chest-dominant overall, head-mix-dominant for high belt above C5) and the 2019 companion paper allows EITHER chest-mix OR head-mix. Claim [20] verifier flags the independent-sphincter vs tongue-root active debate (2024 MRI). Claims [7][8] establish CCM vibrato is non-uniform vs classical, making the direct-vs-emergent teaching question and the scoring approach genuinely open.

## Refuted

- CVT's Overdrive mode is the primary mode for loud/belt singing, described as clear, direct, unrestrained and 'shouty', and is vowel-restricted to only 'EH' (as in 'stay') and 'OH' (as in 'so'). This directly constrains belt-exercise syllable choices.
- The abstract contains NO description of the specific CVT modes (Neutral, Curbing, Overdrive, Edge), nor of belt, twang, dynamics, volume control, or any concrete vocal exercise — confirming this source does not contain primary-source exercise specs for the three target capabilities.
- In Estill, twang is an overlay quality that can be combined with Speech, Falsetto, Belt, or other Qualities — meaning twang is not itself belt but a modifier, which clarifies the belt-vs-twang framing for the app's exercise taxonomy.

## Caveats

- SCORABILITY IS THE DOMINANT CAVEAT and it cuts differently per capability: (a) Belt set-ups and twang drills ARE pitch-scorable because they live on clear sustained vowels and arpeggios — but the app scores PITCH, not the spectral brightness (2–4 kHz) or metallic quality that is the actual belt/twang target, so a quality note should accompany them. (b) Vibrato is fundamentally a pitch OSCILLATION (±~1 semitone) — the app's mean-cents-over-stable-portion scoring will average it out or misread it as instability; only the STRAIGHT-TONE portion of a control exercise is honestly scorable. (c) Messa di voce is NOT scorable at all on this app — it trains loudness (SPL) on a fixed pitch, and the app has no amplitude scorer (only a −45 dB noise gate). SOURCE-STRENGTH CAVEATS: the belt-vowel exercises (Roll 2016) are from a FEMALE Broadway-belt qualitative study of 4 teachers — directly applicable to soprano/alto, transposition to tenor/baritone is a reasonable but unverified extension. The Overdrive/Edge laryngeal study (n=20, CVT-founder co-authored, unreplicated) and the Kochis-Jennings EMG study (n=7, females, EMG not statistically tested) are modest samples. Vibrato rate norms come from CLASSICAL college majors; cross-genre data show the 5–7 Hz band transfers to CCM/rock but CCM vibrato is measurably less regular. GAP: no primary-source riff/run (pentatonic/blues melisma) exercise pattern survived verification — that capability is NOT build-ready from primary sources and any pattern shipped would be app-author-invented, compounded by the app's difficulty pitch-detecting very fast runs. The refuted Overdrive-vowel claim (1-2) means CVT Overdrive's exact vowel restriction is NOT reliably 'EH/OH only' — do not hard-code that constraint.

## Open Questions

- What are the citable, primary-source riff/run agility patterns (specific pentatonic/blues-scale melisma scale-degree sequences) for CCM — and at what tempo can the app's pitch detector reliably score them versus needing follow-along mode? This pass found no verified spec.
- Do the female-derived Broadway belt vowel exercises (nyah/ma-mæ/wee/yay) transpose validly to tenor and baritone parts, or do male belt set-ups use different vowels/passaggio targets (the verified sources are female-Broadway-specific)?
- Should the app build a lightweight AMPLITUDE/loudness envelope scorer (technically feasible per the patent evidence in claim [10]) to unlock messa di voce and dynamics scoring, rather than restricting MDV to unscored follow-along?
- Can vibrato be scored by a DEDICATED oscillation analysis (FFT of the cents-time signal to extract rate ~5–7 Hz and extent ~±100 cents) instead of mean-cents — turning the app's current limitation into a vibrato-specific feature, and would CCM's higher vibrato variability (claim [8]) break a fixed-target scorer?
