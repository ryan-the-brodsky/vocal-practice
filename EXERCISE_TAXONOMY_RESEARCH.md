# Deep Research — CCM Vocal-Exercise Capability Taxonomy

> Generated by the deep-research workflow (adversarial 3-vote verified). Source: app exercise-library expansion + selection-page UX.

## Executive Summary

The verified evidence supports a CCM-oriented capability taxonomy of ~10 categories — SOVT/warm-up & recovery, onset & adduction, registration & mix, range/passaggio navigation, agility, resonance/vowel tuning, dynamics, articulation, vibrato, and stylistic (belt/riffs) — each grounded in primary voice science (Titze on SOVT, Bozeman on passaggio acoustics, Kochis-Jennings and the Journal of Voice multiparametric study on mix). The strongest scientifically-grounded categories are SOVT (Titze 2006/2018: semi-occlusion lowers phonation threshold pressure, reduces collision forces, lowers F1 to ~200 Hz to support mixed registration) and registration/mix (mix is a graded TA-adduction continuum, not a discrete third mechanism, and is aerodynamically/acoustically intermediate between chest and falsetto — validating it as its own trainable capability). Bozeman provides a directly translatable, graded passaggio-navigation exercise progression (leap-and-back on close vowels → stepwise ascent) that maps cleanly to scale-degree descriptors and seeds a range/registration growth path. For competitive UX/IA, market leaders converge on capability/problem-area grouping with "what this builds" labels (VocalBee's 7 categories + goal playlists; Kathleen's ~45 topical playlists; Singing Success's foundation→mastery gated progression; Ken Tamplin's 9-belt color-coded capability ladder) — giving concrete patterns for a benefit-driven selection page and a beginner→advanced guided-path feature. Two claims were refuted (SOVT does NOT reliably increase EGG closed quotient; the Wikipedia "five warm-up objectives" framing did not survive), so the taxonomy should lean on the confirmed mechanisms rather than those framings.

## Verified Findings (10)


### 1. [high]

SOVT (semi-occluded vocal tract) is a scientifically validated, distinct capability category — 'SOVT & warm-up/recovery' — and is the gentlest entry point for register-bridging. Mechanism: a semi-occlusion (lip trill, tongue trill, straw, hum, /ng/ siren) produces steady supraglottal back-pressure that counteracts the tendency to hyper-adduct/press the folds, AND creates favorable (inertive) acoustic impedance that lowers phonation threshold pressure for easier phonation. Straw phonation, lip trill, and tongue trill are the canonical, directly-studied SOVT trio. Existing exercises mapping here: Rossini lip trill, Ng siren (both correctly classified as low-fold-pressure SOVT; both UNSCORABLE → run as unscored follow-along).


**Evidence:** Titze 2006 (JSLHR, primary): semi-occlusion gives 'a steady supraglottal pressure to counteract a tendency to hyper-adduct or press the vocal folds' and 'favorable acoustic impedance above the glottis for low phonation threshold pressure and ease of phonation.' Dargin & Searl 2015 (J Voice) directly studied straw phonation, lip trill, and tongue trill in singers, validating these three as the canonical SOVT category. All claims 3-0.


**Sources:** https://pubs.asha.org/doi/abs/10.1044/1092-4388(2006/035) · https://vocology.utah.edu/_resources/documents/major_benefits_of_sovtes_titze.pdf · https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199714001131


### 2. [high]

SOVT mechanistically supports MIXED REGISTRATION and range extension, not just gentle warm-up — giving it a dual role in the taxonomy (warm-up AND a registration/range tool). Semi-occlusion lowers the first vocal-tract resonance (F1) to ~200 Hz, equalizing reinforcement of low-frequency harmonics (200–1500 Hz), which helps avoid register instabilities and supports mixed registration. During an SOVT pitch glide the folds slightly separate (reducing vibrational amplitude and collision forces), letting lung pressure and F0 be taken high, and the resulting fold-ligament stretching strengthens access to high pitches. This is the voice-science basis for placing the Ng siren and a straw/lip-trill glide as register-bridging tools, not merely warm-ups.


**Evidence:** Titze 2018 (Journal of Singing, NATS, verbatim from PDF): 'The first resonance frequency is lowered to around 200 Hz... The approximately equal reinforcement of harmonics helps to avoid register instabilities and supports mixed registration.' And: 'Vocal fold vibrational amplitude and collision forces are reduced with slightly separated vocal folds, allowing lung pressure and fundamental frequency to be taken high in a pitch glide. The stretching of the vocal folds strengthens the vocal ligament for better control of, and access to, high pitches.' Both claims 3-0.


**Sources:** https://vocology.utah.edu/_resources/documents/major_benefits_of_sovtes_titze.pdf


### 3. [high]

Mix is a legitimate, trainable, CCM-specific capability — its own taxonomy category ('Registration & Mix') — and is physiologically a GRADED CONTINUUM of thyroarytenoid (TA) engagement and vocal-fold adduction, not a discrete third mechanism. At the same pitch, TA activity and adduction increase monotonically across head → headmix → chestmix → chest. CCM/'commercial' chestmix is produced by maintaining or increasing vocal-process adduction and engaging TA more than for head register — a measurable coordination distinct from head voice. This grounds the app's Bub Mix and Head Voice 'vwohm' exercises as training graded TA engagement/adduction rather than flipping a switch. Existing exercises mapping here: Bub Mix Voice (8-5-3-1 'bub'), Goog octave arpeggio (mix through first passaggio), Head Voice 'vwohm'.


**Evidence:** Kochis-Jennings et al. (J Voice, EMG + videonasendoscopy of 7 female singers): 'For same pitch phonation, subjects increased TA muscle activity and vocal fold adduction as they shifted register from head to headmix to chestmix to chest.' And: 'Commercial singers produce chestmix register by maintaining or increasing adduction of the vocal processes (VPs) and by engaging the thyroarytenoid (TA) muscle to a greater degree than they would to produce head register.' Caveat noted in verification: the clean register-specific pattern weakens above ~300 Hz (CT-dominant). Claim on monotonic engagement 2-1; CCM-specific coordination 3-0.


**Sources:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199710002195


### 4. [high]

Mix is aerodynamically, glottally, and acoustically INTERMEDIATE between chest and falsetto — not a simple midpoint average but an efficient, economical phonation mode — reinforcing 'mix' as a distinct capability worth a dedicated category and progression. Mix combines the LOW subglottal pressure of falsetto with the LOW mean airflow of chest; its open quotient (fold-closure state) sits between chest (most closed) and falsetto (least closed); and its harmonic structure is intermediate. These give physiological/acoustic justification for the pedagogical 'blend' concept and for sequencing mix exercises between chest-dominant and head-dominant work.


**Evidence:** Lee et al. multiparametric J Voice study (HSDI + EGG + aerodynamics, PMID 33518476): 'the PSub of the mix was as low as the falsetto, and the MFR of the mix was as low as the chest'; 'the OQ of the mix was higher than that of the chest but lower than that of the falsetto'; 'the harmonic structure was intermediate between the chest and falsetto for mixed register.' Aerodynamic-distinctness claim 2-1 (interpretive 'efficient' tail hedged); OQ and harmonic-structure claims 3-0.


**Sources:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0892199720304860


### 5. [high]

The passaggio 'turning over' is primarily an ACOUSTIC event (the second harmonic H2 passing through the first formant F1), not chiefly a laryngeal-muscle register change — and because F1 varies by vowel, the turnover pitch is vowel-dependent (~one octave below each vowel's F1). Vowel openness predicts turnover location: close [e]/[o] turn at the primo passaggio, [ɛ]/[ø] mid-zone, open [å] near the secondo passaggio; [i] and [u] share the lowest F1. CONSEQUENCE FOR THE APP: vowel choice is a direct lever on passaggio difficulty, so exercises should pair specific vowels with specific register goals (e.g., narrow 'oo'/'ee' to ease early passaggio, open vowels to push the secondo passaggio). This grounds a 'Range Extension / Passaggio Navigation' capability category and explains the design of Goog ('oo'), Bub, and the head-voice slides.


**Evidence:** Bozeman 2010 (Journal of Singing, NATS, verbatim from PDF): the timbral shift occurs 'when the second harmonic (H2) of the voice source passes through the first formant (F1)... approximately one octave below the various first formant locations of each vowel.' Vowel ordering: 'Close [e] and [o] turn over at about the traditional location of the primo passaggio... [ɛ] and [ø]... in the middle of that zone, and [å]... near the secondo passaggio.' '[i] and [u] share the lowest first formant.' Acoustic-event claim 3-0; vowel-openness ordering 2-1 (one terminological slip on 'inverse' flagged but turnover taxonomy verbatim-confirmed). Scoped to MALE voice in source.


**Sources:** https://faculty.lawrence.edu/bozemank/wp-content/uploads/sites/10/2014/03/Bozeman_JOS-2010_JanFeb-.pdf


### 6. [high]

Bozeman provides a concrete, graded passaggio-navigation exercise progression that is DIRECTLY translatable into the app's scale-degree (semitone-offset) descriptors, seeding both a new family of exercises and the ordering of a range/registration growth path. Easiest→hardest: (1) leap from a low open vowel [å] to a high close vowel [u]; (2) leap above and back on one vowel, e.g. do-sol-do = [0,7,0]; (3) leap above the turn then descend stepwise re-opening, e.g. sol-do-ti-la-sol; (4) descend stepwise then reverse and ascend; (5) hardest — pure stepwise ascent through the passaggio, do-re-mi-fa-sol-fa-mi-re-do = [0,2,4,5,7,5,4,2,0]. These are PITCH-SCORABLE on sustained vowels (the app can score sung [u]/[å] scale tones); only buzzy/SOVT or very-fast variants would be unscored. This fills the current gap of a deliberate stepwise-through-the-bridge exercise.


**Evidence:** Bozeman 2010 'Exercise Progression' section (verbatim via pdftotext): 'from easiest to most challenging: 1) ...leap from a low open vowel [å] to a high close vowel [u]. 2) ...leap above a point of turning and back on the same vowel... such as do-sol-do... 3) ...leap above a point of turning, and then descend with stepwise movement, gradually re-opening: sol-do-ti-la-sol... 5) ...ascend stepwise... do-re-mi-fa-sol-fa-mi-re-do. (This is the most challenging...).' Claim 3-0. Note: source scoped to male voice; generalize with care for soprano/alto.


**Sources:** https://faculty.lawrence.edu/bozemank/wp-content/uploads/sites/10/2014/03/Bozeman_JOS-2010_JanFeb-.pdf


### 7. [high]

Methods genuinely DISAGREE on what 'mix' is and how to categorize sound, which the taxonomy and any 'what this builds' labels must navigate. CVT (Complete Vocal Technique) classifies ALL singing into exactly four vocal modes — Neutral, Curbing, Overdrive, Edge (formerly 'Belting') — graded along a single 'metallic content' axis (Neutral = non-metallic, Curbing = half-metallic, Overdrive + Edge = full-metallic). This is a mode/sound-quality taxonomy, NOT a chest/mix/head register taxonomy, and CVT notably does not use 'mix' as a primary category — contrasting with SLS/Singing Success and the voice-science continuum view (mix = graded TA/adduction). For a CCM library this means 'belt' is better framed as a metallic/full-engagement sound goal (CVT Edge/Overdrive) layered on top of the registration continuum, rather than a separate register.


**Evidence:** CVT UK (primary, corroborated by Complete Vocal Institute + voicescience.org lexicon + Wikipedia): 'The use of the voice can be divided into four vocal modes: Neutral, Curbing, Overdrive and Edge.' Neutral 'the only non-metallic mode'; Curbing 'the only half-metallic mode'; Overdrive 'one of two full-metallic modes'; Edge (formerly 'Belting') 'the other full-metallic mode.' Peer-reviewed J Voice studies ('Curbing—The Metallic Mode In-between') corroborate the metallic grading. Both claims 3-0.


**Sources:** https://www.completevocaltechnique.co.uk/what-is-the-complete-vocal-technique/

## Refuted / Did Not Survive Verification

- SOVT exercises measurably increase EGG closed quotient (greater vocal-fold contact/adduction) along with mean airflow and sound pressure level immediately afterward, supporting the pedagogical claim that SOVT warm-ups improve vocal-fold closure and economy — the rationale for placing the Rossini lip trill and Ng siren in a 'SOVT / warm-up' capability category.
- Vocal warm-ups serve five distinct functional objectives: whole-body physical activation, breath preparation, articulator/resonator preparation, register transition (spoken to singing), and material-specific preparation. This maps directly onto a capability taxonomy for organizing exercises.

## Caveats

- SOURCE QUALITY: The taxonomy's scientific spine is strong — Titze (SOVT, JSLHR 2006 + Journal of Singing 2018), Bozeman (passaggio acoustics, Journal of Singing 2010), Kochis-Jennings (J Voice EMG), and Lee et al. (J Voice multiparametric, PMID 33518476) are all primary peer-reviewed sources with verbatim-confirmed quotes. Method/curriculum and competitor-IA claims rest on VENDOR PRIMARY PAGES (CVT, KTVA store, Singing Success, VocalBee, Kathleen App Store) — appropriate for descriptive 'how they structure/label things' claims, but these are marketing-context sources, not independent efficacy validation; treat their pedagogical EFFICACY as unverified, only their IA/structure as confirmed. TWO REFUTED CLAIMS to avoid building on: (1) SOVT does NOT reliably increase EGG closed quotient (vocal-fold contact) immediately afterward (0-3) — so do not claim SOVT 'improves closure'; its validated benefits are reduced collision force, lower PTP, and F1 lowering. (2) The 'five functional warm-up objectives' Wikipedia framing did NOT survive (0-3) — do not use it to structure the warm-up category. SCOPE LIMITS: Bozeman's vowel-turnover ordering and exercise progression are explicitly derived for the MALE voice; transposing to soprano/alto needs care (F1/passaggio locations differ). The mix EMG findings (Kochis-Jennings) are female-only, N=7, and the clean register-specific TA/CT pattern weakens above ~300 Hz — so 'mix as graded TA engagement' is best stated for the lower-to-mid range. TIME-SENSITIVITY: competitor product details (exact category counts, course volumes, pricing, Kathleen's playlist count which itself drifts 'over 30' to 'over 60') are snapshots that change with app/course updates; re-verify before quoting specifics in shipped copy. METHOD DISAGREEMENT IS REAL: CVT (4 metallic modes, no 'mix' primary), SLS/Singing Success ('mix' as blend of head+chest), and the voice-science continuum view (mix = graded TA/adduction) genuinely conflict on what 'mix' and 'belt' are — the app's labels should pick one framing (recommended: the continuum view for tagging, with 'belt' as a metallic/full-engagement sound goal) and stay internally consistent rather than mixing vocabularies.

## Open Questions

- Female-voice (soprano/alto) passaggio and mix evidence: Bozeman's vowel-turnover progression is male-scoped and Kochis-Jennings is female-only but lower-range — what is the validated vowel/scale-degree mapping for soprano/alto passaggio navigation, given the app's existing soprano/alto ranges that already peak at A5?
- Scorability boundary for new exercises: the report confirms sustained-vowel passaggio exercises are pitch-scorable and SOVT/buzzy ones are not, but where exactly does 'very fast run' cross into unscorable for the app's pattern-alignment detector (segment ≥80ms / ≥75¢ stable) — this gates whether agility/riffs-and-runs exercises can be scored or must be follow-along.
- Belt/twang as a capability: the confirmed evidence covers mix, SOVT, passaggio, and registration well, but no primary source survived specifically operationalizing BELT or TWANG (Estill aryepiglottic narrowing) as a scale-degree-pattern exercise — what concrete, scorable descriptor builds belt/twang for CCM beyond the existing 'nay' onset exercise?
- Vibrato and dynamics (messa di voce) exercise design: VocalBee lists vibrato as a category and Singing Success sells Mastering Vibrato, but no primary mechanism/exercise-design claim for vibrato or messa di voce survived verification — these capability categories are currently asserted by market convention only and need a pedagogy/voice-science backing pass before building descriptors.
- Empirical sequencing/'graduate when…' thresholds: competitor curricula confirm gated foundation→mastery progressions exist, but none provide measurable graduation criteria (e.g., accuracy %, cents tolerance, range milestones) the app could compute from its own scoring — what concrete, app-measurable signals should gate progression between growth paths?
